# Bank Loan

Description: A simple bank loan is the most traditional way to finance an energy-efficiency project. The project owner (client or ESCO) borrows money from a commercial bank to cover part or all of the CAPEX and repays the loan through regular debt payments (principal + interest) over a fixed period. The repayment usually comes from the energy savings generated by the project, but the bank generally does not directly rely on those savings as security — it relies on the borrower’s creditworthiness and collateral.

Lender: Bank
Good for borrowers that are: any

Project size: any

Additional inputs:
- repayment term (how many years will the loan be repayed in)
- loan ammount

Advantages:
- can have lower interest rates 
- wide availability that accomodate any project size

Disatvantages:
- credit history/ checks
- payment needs to take place independently of the savings achieved in the end
- slow approval process
- the risk is entrirely up to the borrower

# Crowdfunding Mechanisms

## Equity Crowfunding

Description: Equity crowdfunding allows a project developer (e.g., an ESCO or energy project developer) to raise capital from many small investors through an online platform. Instead of lending money, investors receive a share of the project and are paid a percentage of the profits generated by the energy savings.

Lender: Crowd investors through a crowdfunding platform

Good for borrowers that are: ESCOs, project developers, startups without access to bank loans

Project size: small-medium

Additional inputs:
- platform fee (% of equity accumulated, e.g 0.04 -> 4%)
- crowd share (% owned by the crowd, e.g 0.4 -> 40%)

Advantages:
- no fixed repayment obligations
- investors share the financial risk of the project
- good option when bank financing is not available
- no collateral required

Disatvantages:
- profits must be shared with investors
- platform fees can be relatively high
- requires investor interest to successfully raise capital
- interest rates are usually higher than bank loans
- more administrative work (reporting to investors)

## Lending Crowfunding

Description: Lending crowdfunding (crowdlending) works like a traditional loan, but the capital comes from many small investors through an online platform. The borrower must repay the loan with interest over a fixed period, regardless of how the project performs.

Lender: Crowd investors through a crowdfunding platform

Good for borrowers that are: SMEs, ESCOs, small project developers

Project size: small-medium

Additional inputs:
- platform fee (% of equity accumulated, e.g 0.04 -> 4%)
- loan term (in years (must be at least 1))
- fixed interest rate (e.g., 0.05 for 5%)
- crowdfunding capital raised (loan) 

Advantages:
- easier access to capital than bank loans
- borrower keeps full ownership of the project
- fixed and predictable repayment schedule
- faster financing process

Disatvantages:
- interest rates are usually higher than bank loans
- repayment is required even if savings are lower than expected
- platform fees apply
- campaign must attract enough investors

## Royalty Crowdfunding

Description: Royalty crowdfunding allows investors to finance a project in exchange for a percentage of the project's revenue (energy savings) for a fixed number of years. Investors are paid from the gross savings before operating costs, while the ESCO covers maintenance expenses.

Lender: Crowd investors through a crowdfunding platform

Good for borrowers that are: startups, ESCOs, small project developers

Project size: small-medium

Additional inputs:
- platform fee (% of equity accumulated, e.g 0.04 -> 4%)
- term (number of years royalty is paid (must be less than project lifetime))
- fixed royalty rate (% of gross revenue paid to crowd as royalty e.g., 0.05 for 5%)
- crowdfunding capital raised 

Advantages:
- no fixed loan repayment required
- no equity dilution (investors do not own the project)
- payments adjust with project performance
- easier access to capital for new companies

Disatvantages:
- investors receive revenue before operating costs
- can reduce long-term project profitability
- platform fees apply
- less common than other crowdfunding models

# ESCO Funding

## First-Out Contract

Description: In a First-Out contract, the ESCO pays the full cost of the energy efficiency project and recovers its investment from the energy savings generated by the project. The ESCO receives all available savings until the investment is fully repaid. After that point, the client keeps all future savings.

Lender: ESCO

Good for borrowers that are: building owners without capital (large buildings)

Project size: medium-large

Additional inputs:
(no additional inputs needed)

Advantages:
- no upfront investment required by the client
- ESCO carries most financial risk
- suitable for large efficiency projects

Disatvantages:
- client receives no savings during repayment period
- ESCO financing can be expensive
- requires reliable measurement of energy savings
- contract negotiation can be complex as ESCOs require low risk

## EPC Shared Savings

Description: In a shared savings contract, the ESCO finances the project and the energy savings are shared between the ESCO and the client based on an agreed percentage. Both parties benefit from the savings during the entire contract period.

Lender: ESCO

Good for borrowers that are: building owners, municipalities, commercial buildings

Project size: medium-large

Additional inputs:
- ESCO percentage (% of savings paid to ESCO (e.g., 0.30 for 30%))
- contract term (number of years contract is active)

Advantages:
- no upfront investment required
- client benefits from savings immediately
- risk is shared between ESCO and client
- ESCO has strong incentive to maximize savings

Disatvantages:
- client shares savings with ESCO for the contract duration
- effective financing cost may be higher than bank loans
- requires measurement and verification of savings
- contracts can be complex as ESCOs require low risk

## EPC Guaranteed Savings

Description: In a guaranteed savings contract, the client finances the project (usually with a bank loan) while the ESCO guarantees that the project will achieve a minimum level of energy savings. If savings fall below the guaranteed level, the ESCO compensates the difference.

Lender: Bank or external lender

Good for borrowers that are: municipalities, large companies, organizations with access to loans

Project size: municipalities, large companies, organizations with access to loans

Additional inputs:
- guaranteed savings ammount (in money)
- loan term (in years)

Advantages:
- lower financing cost compared to ESCO financing
- client keeps most of the energy savings
- performance guarantee reduces technical risk
- well-suited for public sector projects

Disatvantages:
- client must obtain financing
- requires detailed measurement and verification

# Bonds

## Green Bond with Bullet Repayment

Description: A green bond allows organizations to raise capital from investors to finance environmentally beneficial projects. The issuer pays regular interest payments, while the full principal is repaid at maturity (bullet repayment).

Lender: Institutional investors

Good for borrowers that are: large companies, municipalities, utilities

Project size: large

Additional inputs:
- green bond proceeds (capital provided)
- loan term (in years)
- fixed interest rate
- O&M for green bond

Advantages:
- access to large capital amounts
- often lower interest rates due to ESG demand
- suitable for long-term infrastructure projects
- attracts institutional investors

Disatvantages:
- high transaction and certification costs
- complex issuance process
- requires strong creditworthiness
- large repayment at maturity

## Green Bond Loan

Description: This structure is similar to a traditional loan financed through green bond proceeds. The borrower repays interest and principal gradually over the bond term, reducing the outstanding balance each year.

Lender: Institutional investors

Good for borrowers that are: large companies, municipalities, utilities

Project size: large

Standard inputs:
- capex
- annual energy savings
- annual maintenace cost
- project lifetime 

Additional inputs:
- green bond proceeds (capital provided)
- loan term (in years)
- fixed interest rate
- O&M for green bond

Advantages:
- gradual repayment reduces refinancing risk
- access to large sustainable investment funds
- suitable for large energy efficiency programs
- stable and predictable financing structure

Disatvantages:
- high issuance and reporting costs
- requires strong credit profile
- complex financial structuring

# On-Bill

Description: On-bill financing allows energy efficiency upgrades to be repaid through the electricity bill. The financing provider covers the investment and the customer repays it through a charge added to their utility bill.

Lender: Utility company or third-party lender

Good for borrowers that are: households, small businesses

Project size: small

Standard inputs:
- capex
- annual energy savings
- annual maintenace cost
- project lifetime 

Additional inputs:
- loan term (in years)
- fixed interest rate

Advantages:
- no upfront investment required
- repayment integrated into electricity bill
- easier access for customers with limited credit
- often designed so savings offset repayments

Disatvantages:
- depends on utility programs and regulation
- limited to smaller projects
- financing cost may vary
- not widely available in all regions

# Operational Lease

Description: In operational leasing, a leasing company purchases the energy efficiency equipment and leases it to the client for a fixed period. The client pays regular lease payments to use the equipment but does not own it.

Lender: Leasing company

Good for borrowers that are: SMEs, commercial buildings, industrial facilities

Project size: small-medium

Standard inputs:
- annual energy savings
- annual maintenace cost
- project lifetime 

Additional inputs:
- leasing term (in years)
- lease payment

Advantages:
- no upfront capital required
- maintenance is often included
- easier technology upgrades

Disatvantages:
- client does not own the equipment
- total cost may be higher than direct purchase
- contracts may limit equipment modifications
